# myco-ics — Incident Command over the mesh

> **Status: POC / proposal** for a not-yet-built app. Voice is "the app will…".
> Open questions are marked **TBD / open**. myco-ics is an **nsite that runs on
> Myco**; it depends on Myco capabilities that are flagged as *later milestones*
> (a signing-and-relay surface for nsites, and app-kind propagation — see §11).
> It does **not** run on the pure-static v1 nsite sandbox as-is.

myco-ics is an [nsite](../../docs/design/concepts.md) — a static web app
distributed like any other Myco app — that implements the behavioural skeleton
of **ICS (Incident Command System)**, the emergency-response coordination
protocol used by fire, rescue, and disaster organisations worldwide. Its purpose
is to demonstrate one thing: a **spontaneous, cryptographically-verifiable chain
of command** forming with **no internet and no infrastructure**, over a Myco BLE
mesh.

Myco is a dumb replication layer — it floods signed Nostr events and serves
content-addressed blobs between devices, with **no knowledge of authority or
hierarchy**. myco-ics builds the entire authority model *inside the app*, from
Nostr keypairs and public attestation events. The mesh carries the bytes; the app
decides what they mean. This is the same division of labour Myco already draws
between its transport and its content layer (see
[concepts.md § Pillars of Propagation](../../docs/design/concepts.md)).

---

## 1. Core thesis (what the POC proves)

Five people in a room, airplane mode on, Bluetooth on. One declares an incident
and is its Incident Commander. The others join and request roles under someone
already in the chain. Each leader accepts or denies. Within two minutes every
screen shows the **same** chain of command, each link verified back to the IC's
key — formed with zero infrastructure.

Then a message flows: the IC pushes an objective *down* the chain, a field member
logs activity *up* the chain, and a supervisor delegates an assignment to a
division. All of it floods over the mesh, all of it cryptographically attributable
to a verified role.

That is the whole POC. Everything below is how.

---

## 2. Where this sits in the Myco stack

myco-ics rides the **same two-layer split** Myco already defines for offline
propagation ([propagation.md § 1](../../docs/design/propagation.md)). Nothing here
changes the transport; it is all content-layer.

| | Layer A — FIPS transport | Layer B — nsite/relay layer |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Provides | live-path, multi-hop, encrypted delivery between currently-connected nodes | store-and-forward of signed events + content-addressed blobs; survives partition |
| myco-ics uses it for | the raw BLE/mesh hop under everything | **all ICS state**: role/attestation/form events (flooded) and voice/photo blobs (pulled) |

Concretely, ICS state decomposes onto Myco's existing propagation primitives
exactly as an nsite does:

- **ICS events** (incident, role-request, attestation, objectives, assignment,
  activity-log) are small, self-authenticating signed Nostr events. They
  **flood** neighbour-to-neighbour the same way an author-signed manifest floods
  — Plumtree eager-push via the propagator, TTL-bounded, dedup'd by event id
  ([propagation.md § 2](../../docs/design/propagation.md)).
- **ICS blobs** (a voice note, a photo of a map) are **pulled on demand** by
  sha256 from a holder in your circle, exactly like an nsite's blobs — never
  flooded. *(Blobs are a later milestone; see §12.)*

So myco-ics is not a new propagation mechanism. It is a new *application of the
existing one* — which is precisely why it is a good demonstration of the flooding
principle (§9).

## 3. Identity and authority — three separate keys

Myco is already rigorous about not conflating key roles (see
[concepts.md § Device identity vs nsite author identity](../../docs/design/concepts.md)).
myco-ics adds a **third** key and keeps all three strictly apart:

| Key | Whose | Role | Held by |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **device key** | this Myco device | mesh/relay address (`<npub>.fips`), BLE link auth | the Myco app |
| **nsite author key** | whoever authored the myco-ics app | signs the myco-ics **app bundle** so it propagates as an installable nsite | external tooling, off-device |
| **participant key** | this **person** in this incident | signs every ICS event this person authors (role, attestation, forms) | **myco-ics itself**, generated on first launch, stored in the app's own origin storage |

The **participant key is generated by myco-ics and is completely independent** of
the device key and the author key. It inherits nothing from the Myco node — a
person's identity in the chain of command is theirs, not their handset's. (This
is the load-bearing correction: *no Myco identity is reused.*) The app could run
against any Nostr relay; Myco just happens to be the mesh underneath.

From these keys the authority model is built out of two event-level primitives:

- **Role declaration** — a signed event: "I am *Operations Chief*." Self-asserted,
  and **meaningless on its own**.
- **Attestation** — the superior publishes a signed event referencing the
  subordinate's participant pubkey and role: "I confirm this key is Operations
  Chief under me." This is the **trust primitive**. It is what turns a
  self-assertion into a verified position.

The **chain of command** is the directed graph of attestation events, **rooted at
the IC's participant key**. The IC is self-appointed (first mover) and announced
by the incident event; every other role is valid **only if its attestation chain
traces back to that root**. No central authority, no server, no pre-shared secret
— the chain is fully verifiable from the event graph alone, offline (§6).

Crucially, Myco peering does **not** confer authority. Being in someone's circle
only means the mesh will carry your events to them; it says nothing about rank.
Rank lives entirely in the attestation graph the app interprets.

## 4. Incident scoping

Every ICS event carries the **incident id** — the Nostr event id of the
`ics:incident` announcement that started it — in an indexed `["e", "<incident_id>"]`
tag. That id is the natural namespace for everything: attestations, forms, and
org-chart queries all filter on it (`{"#e": ["<incident_id>"]}`).

Because the namespace is just an event id, **multiple incidents coexist on one
mesh by default**. A device maintains one event graph per incident id it has seen;
there is no coordination between incidents and no special handling — the design
supports concurrency for free. The "active incidents" list is simply the set of
`ics:incident` events a device has received.

## 5. Event kinds

All ICS events are signed Nostr events authored with a **participant key** (§3),
published to the local embedded relay (`ws://localhost:4869`), and replicated by
the propagator (§9). Every event carries `["e", "<incident_id>"]`; events
addressed at a specific person also carry `["p", "<pubkey>"]`. Both are standard
single-letter indexed tags, so the relay can filter by incident and by target.

### Hierarchy events

| Kind | Name | Class | Publisher | Key content / tags |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `9470` | incident announcement | regular | IC | incident name; its own id **is** the incident id |
| `9471` | role request | regular | candidate | requested role; `["p", <superior>]` |
| `9472` | attestation (accept) | regular | superior | `["p", <subordinate>]`; role |
| `9473` | denial | regular | superior | `["e", <role-request-id>]` |

### Message / form events

| Kind | Name | Class | Publisher | Flows |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `39470` | ICS-202 Incident Objectives | addressable (`d` = incident id) | IC / Planning | **down** — objectives for the current operational period |
| `9474` | ICS-204 Assignment List | regular | Operations / Division | **down** — tasks to a division; `["p", <target>]` |
| `9475` | ICS-214 Activity Log | regular | any attested member | **up** — timestamped record of an action |
| `9476` | ICS-204 status / comment | regular | assignee / issuing leader | **up** — acks, progress, and completion on a `9474`; `["e", <assignment>, "", "reply"]`, `["status", <state>]` |

**Down / up in one line.** A `9470` incident and `39470` objectives fan out to
everyone; a `9474` assignment is addressed at a division and read authoritatively
by anyone who can verify the author's role; a `9475` log entry is authored at the
edge and pulled inward by superiors building the incident picture. Together the
three forms demonstrate **bidirectional** propagation through the attested
hierarchy — the point of the POC beyond hierarchy *formation*.

Objectives are **addressable** (keyed on `d = incident id`) so a newer 202
supersedes the last — modelling "current objectives." Per-operational-period
objectives would later extend the `d` tag to `<incident_id>:<period>`. Everything
else is a **regular** (immutable, append-only) event, because an incident record
must not be silently rewritten.

> **Kind numbers are provisional.** `9470–9476` (regular) and `39470`
> (addressable) are illustrative POC values chosen for their Nostr classes
> (regular = stored/immutable, addressable = replaceable-by-`d`; see
> [nostr-kinds.md](../../docs/reference/nostr-kinds.md)). If this graduates past
> POC they must be registered there so they never collide with the manifest
> (`15128`/`35128`) or FIPS discovery kinds.

### Worked example — an attestation (kind 9472)

```jsonc
{
  "kind": 9472,
  "pubkey": "<superior participant pubkey>",
  "created_at": 1751328000,
  "content": "",
  "tags": [
    ["e", "<incident_id>"],                 // which incident
    ["p", "<subordinate participant pubkey>"], // who is being attested
    ["role", "Division Supervisor"],        // as what
    ["e", "<role-request_id>", "", "reply"] // answering this request
  ],
  "id": "…",
  "sig": "…"                                 // signed by the superior
}
```

Anyone can check the `sig` against the superior's key, then recurse on the
superior (§6). The subordinate cannot forge it — they do not hold the superior's
secret key — which is the same self-authenticating-data property Myco relies on
for nsite manifests ([propagation.md § 4](../../docs/design/propagation.md)).

## 6. Attestation chain verification

To verify that participant pubkey **P** holds role **R** in incident **I**:

1. Find the `9472` attestation where `#p = P`, `role = R`, `#e = I`, and check
   its signature is valid for its author **Q** (P's claimed superior).
2. If **Q** is the IC of **I** — i.e. Q authored the `9470` incident event whose
   id is **I** — the chain is **verified**.
3. Otherwise, recurse: verify **Q** holds some superior role in **I**.
4. If the recursion reaches a break (a missing or badly-signed attestation, or a
   cycle), P is **unverified** — shown as *pending* or *broken*, never as an
   authoritative role.

The IC's authority is **axiomatic**: whoever signed the `9470` incident event is
the root of trust for that incident. Participants choose which incidents to join,
and joining an incident *is* the act of accepting its IC as the root. There is no
appeal to any authority outside the event graph.

## 7. Role taxonomy (POC)

Minimal ICS hierarchy, enough to show a multi-level chain:

```
Incident Commander (IC)
└── Section Chief          (Operations / Planning / Logistics)
    └── Division Supervisor
        └── Unit Leader
```

Four levels give a three-hop attestation chain to verify. Role names are
free-form strings in the POC; the app does not enforce the ICS taxonomy or
per-role permissions beyond "a leader can attest people under themselves."

## 8. Flows

### 8.1 Start an incident

- The IC taps **Start incident** and names it.
- myco-ics ensures a participant key exists (generating one on first launch).
- It publishes a `9470` incident event to the local relay; the propagator floods
  it outward.
- The IC enters the command view; their own key is the root of the chain.

### 8.2 Discover and join

- A device receives `9470` events from the mesh; **Active incidents** lists them
  (name, IC pubkey, age).
- The user picks an incident, a role, and a superior from the current org chart
  (or requests directly under the IC), and myco-ics publishes a `9471` role
  request tagged `["p", <superior>]`.

### 8.3 Accept / deny

- The addressed superior sees *"Carol requests Division Supervisor under you."*
- **Accept** → publish a `9472` attestation. **Deny** → publish a `9473` denial.
- The decision floods the mesh; every device updates its org chart. This
  accept/deny gate is the app's only admission control — **leaders alone decide
  who joins under them** (there is no ambient auto-accept).

### 8.4 Org chart view

- Built entirely from the local event graph — no server query, fully offline.
- Each node shows role, display name, truncated pubkey, and attestation status
  (*verified / pending / denied*), and updates live as events arrive.
- Tapping a node shows its full verification path to the IC (§6).

### 8.5 Messages up and down

- **Down:** the IC publishes a `39470` objective; it reaches every participant.
  A Section Chief publishes a `9474` assignment tagged at a division; that
  division reads it as authoritative once the author's role verifies.
- **Up:** a Unit Leader publishes a `9475` activity-log entry; superiors, who
  track their subordinates' keys, pull it in to build the picture.
- **Working an assignment.** The assignee (or the issuing leader) posts `9476`
  updates that **reply** to the `9474` — acknowledging, reporting progress, or
  marking it **complete/blocked** via a `["status", <state>]` tag — and may
  attach a comment. The latest status wins; comments accumulate as a thread. This
  closes the loop a paper ICS-204 leaves to radio and the 214 log: the division
  reports back **up** the same attested chain that issued the order.

## 9. Propagation — the flooding principle applied

This is the section that ties myco-ics back to *why Myco exists*. The behaviour
the user cares about — **"a leader shares a message; a subordinate pulls it from
their circle, re-publishes it, which triggers the next tier to pull it from
theirs"** — is not new machinery. It is Myco's existing hybrid propagation
([propagation.md § 2](../../docs/design/propagation.md)) with ICS events in the
role manifests play:

1. **Flood the signed event (eager-push).** A leader signs an ICS event and
   publishes it to the local relay. The **propagator** re-publishes it to each
   connected peer relay (the leader's circle), source-excluded. Each recipient
   stores it and re-publishes onward — the same Plumtree eager-push, TTL, and
   16-byte-event-id dedup seen-set the manifest flood uses. The *event* is small
   and self-authenticating, so it ripples out cheaply and its authorship survives
   every hop unmodified.
2. **Pull the blob on demand.** If the event references a voice note or photo
   (by sha256), a receiving device **pulls the blob** from a holder in its circle
   — the leader, or a peer who already pulled it — over `<npub_holder>.fips:24242`,
   verifies the hash, and caches it. Having cached it, that device is now a
   **holder** and serves it to *its* subordinates, whose devices see the same
   event ripple down and pull from *their* circle. Content follows demand down the
   tree; the mesh never floods the heavy bytes.
3. **Repair gaps (lazy-pull).** Between neighbours, negentropy (NIP-77)
   reconciliation ([propagation.md § 5](../../docs/design/propagation.md)) closes
   any holes left by a missed flood — so a member who was briefly out of range
   converges on the full incident graph when they reconnect.

Because every ICS event is self-authenticating and every blob is
content-addressed, **any relay path is trustworthy** — a malicious or faulty
intermediate can withhold, delay, or reorder, but cannot forge an attestation or
tamper with an objective without detection ([propagation.md § 4](../../docs/design/propagation.md)).
That is exactly what lets the chain of command survive a partitioned, ad-hoc,
no-internet mesh.

## 10. Trust and threat model

Authority is the attestation chain rooted at the incident's IC key (§3, §6); the
data is self-authenticating, so trust is in **keys and signatures, not in the
path**. This bounds the threats a POC must reason about:

| Threat | Mitigation |
| --- | --- |
| **Forged rank** — someone claims a role they were not given | Roles are only authoritative with a valid `9472` chain to the IC (§6); a bare self-declaration is shown as unverified. |
| **Impersonating a leader** — publishing attestations as someone else | Every event is signed by a participant key; a forged signature fails verification and is dropped. |
| **Malicious relay/holder** — a device on the path tampers or drops | Cannot corrupt without detection (signatures + sha256); can only withhold/delay, which negentropy repair (§9) and multi-path flooding mitigate. |
| **Sybil subordinates** — flooding role requests | The **leader accept/deny gate** (§8.3) is the admission control; nothing joins a chain without a human attestation. |
| **Fake incident / fake IC** | Participants choose which incident to join; the IC's key is established out-of-band (they are in the room). Joining is consent to that root. |
| **Replay of old events** | Event ids + the dedup seen-set (§9); addressable objectives supersede by `created_at`. |

**Explicitly out of the POC threat model:** key loss and **IC succession** (an
injured commander whose phone is gone). The purely-cryptographic model has no
answer to this without a human-shaped quorum mechanism; it is deferred (§12).

## 11. What myco-ics needs from Myco

myco-ics cannot run on the **pure-static v1 nsite sandbox** — concepts.md is
explicit that v1 nsites have "no privileged access" and that a capability API is a
later milestone ([concepts.md § What an nsite is](../../docs/design/concepts.md)).
myco-ics is a concrete **driving use case** for exactly that API. It needs three
capabilities, each mapping to an already-flagged Myco open item:

1. **A signing identity.** The app must hold/generate its participant key and sign
   events. It can do this in-app (a Nostr library in the WebView, key in origin
   storage) — no Myco secret is ever exposed — but the sandbox must permit it.
2. **Local relay access.** The app must open a WebSocket to the embedded relay
   (`ws://localhost:4869`) to publish and subscribe. Whether a sandboxed nsite
   (loaded from `http://<host>.nsite`) may reach the relay port is a **sandbox /
   capability question** owned by [app-shell.md](../../docs/design/app-shell.md)
   and the "nsite capability API" roadmap item.
3. **App-kind propagation.** The propagator today floods only manifest kinds
   (`15128`/`35128`). For ICS kinds (§5) to hop multi-hop, the propagator must be
   permitted to subscribe/forward them. This is precisely the **nsite-scoped
   propagation (capability-gated)** open question in
   [propagation.md § Open questions](../../docs/design/propagation.md): *"a
   kind-1 client nsite asking the propagator to also reconcile/forward"* app
   kinds, **default-deny**, with explicit limits on kinds, rate, storage, and
   scope, behind user consent.

Positioning myco-ics this way keeps the doc honest: it does not pretend to run on
v1, and it gives the capability-API work a real application to be designed
against.

## 12. Out of scope (POC)

- **Voice / photo blobs.** The pull-on-demand half (§9 step 2) — described so the
  propagation story is complete, but not built in the POC. First milestone after
  hierarchy formation.
- **Key recovery / IC succession** (§10) — needs a human-shaped quorum/co-sign
  mechanism; deferred.
- **Per-role permission enforcement** beyond the accept/deny gate.
- **Full ICS form set** (205 comms plan, 206 medical, 209 status summary, 211
  check-in, 215 planning worksheet). The POC ships 202/204/214 as the
  representative down/up/lateral trio.
- **Operational-period lifecycle** (briefing cycle, period rollover).

## 13. Demo script

*All devices in airplane mode, Bluetooth on.*

1. **Alice** opens myco-ics, taps *Start incident: Field Exercise Alpha*. She is
   IC; her participant key is the root.
2. **Bob** sees the incident appear, requests *Operations Chief* under Alice.
   Alice accepts. Bob is attested Operations Chief.
3. **Carol** requests *Division Supervisor* under Bob. Bob accepts.
4. **Dave** requests *Unit Leader* under Carol. Carol accepts.
5. Every screen shows the same four-level tree. Dave taps his own node — the app
   traces `Dave ← Carol ← Bob ← Alice (IC, root)`, each hop signature-verified,
   fully offline.
6. **Alice** publishes an ICS-202: *"Objective: establish comms perimeter by
   1400."* It floods **down** — Bob, Carol, and Dave all see it.
7. **Dave** logs an ICS-214: *"1312 — comms post established, sector 4."* It
   floods **up** — Carol, Bob, and Alice see it in the incident log.
8. **Bob** publishes an ICS-204 addressed to Carol's division: *"Assign two units
   to north gate."* Carol's assignment list updates.

Chain formed, objectives pushed down, field activity logged up, assignments
delegated — all offline, all cryptographically attributable to a verified role.
That is the flooding principle carrying a live command structure with no internet.
